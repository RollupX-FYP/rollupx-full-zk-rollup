\section{Background and Related Work}

\subsection{Ethereum Scalability and Rollups}

Ethereum's rollup-centric scaling roadmap relies on Layer-2 systems that move execution off-chain while using Layer-1 for settlement, verification, and data availability~\cite{ethereum_scaling_docs,ethereum_data_availability_docs}. In this model, rollups amortize Layer-1 costs across many off-chain transactions while retaining the base chain as the security and settlement layer.

ZK-Rollups strengthen this model by using validity proofs to attest to the correctness of off-chain state transitions. Depending on the system design, these proofs may be based on SNARK- or STARK-style constructions, including pairing-based arguments such as Groth16 and transparent proof systems such as STARKs~\cite{groth2016snark,ben2018stark}. This allows Layer-1 contracts to verify compact proof artifacts instead of re-executing every transaction.

\subsection{Performance Bottlenecks and Benchmarking}

Despite their scalability benefits, ZK-Rollups introduce performance bottlenecks that depend on both cryptographic and systems-level design choices. Proof generation can become a major computational bottleneck, especially as batch size and circuit complexity increase~\cite{habib2025bottlenecks}. At the same time, batching decisions affect queueing delay, finalization time, and the degree to which Layer-1 submission costs are amortized across transactions~\cite{chaliasos2024benchmarking}.

Data availability is another critical design dimension. Posting rollup data as calldata provides strong availability through Ethereum but can be expensive. EIP-4844 introduces blob-carrying transactions that provide cheaper temporary data availability for rollups. Blob contents are not directly accessible to EVM execution, but commitments to blob data are accessible, making blobs suitable for rollup data posting rather than general contract execution data~\cite{eip4844,ethereum_data_availability_docs}. This changes the cost structure of rollup submission and motivates separate evaluation of calldata and blob-based DA paths~\cite{saif2024dataavailability,lavaur2023modular}.

Sequencer design also affects rollup performance and fairness. Since sequencers determine transaction ordering and batch construction, ordering policies can influence latency distribution, inclusion fairness, and economic behavior~\cite{motepalli2023sequencers}. Existing studies analyze production rollup traces, public datasets, or deployed systems to understand these behaviors at scale~\cite{silva2025zksyncdataset,chaliasos2024benchmarking}.

\subsection{Research Gap}

Prior work provides valuable insight into rollup economics, data availability, sequencing, and production-trace behavior. However, production rollups are not primarily designed as experimental platforms, and their internal policies are difficult to modify under a fixed benchmark harness. There remains a need for a compact modular prototype that supports controlled experiments over batching, sequencing, data availability, and proof-delay parameters under identical workloads.
